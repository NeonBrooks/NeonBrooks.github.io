I am a postdoctoral researcher working with Dr. Elizabeth Spelke in the Laboratory for Developmental Studies at Harvard University. Through my research, I investigate the ways that we represent complex and abstract information mentally. I am particularly interested in how and when we make use of analog forms of representation like action, gesture, and mental imagery; and how these might work together with symbolic forms of representation like languge.

My work at Harvard focused on two lines of research: in the first, I am investigating how infants interpret their own actions on the world, and what information they use to learn from these actions. In the second, I am investigating the roles that action, gesture, and language can play on children's understanding of matehematical and geometric concepts. In this line of work, I am particularly interested in how we can improve children's learning of difficult mathematical and spatial concepts, both in the US and in India.

My doctoral dissertation focused on uncovering the role of gesture in cognition by making use of particularly useful case study: children in India who solve arithmetic problems using the Mental Abacus technique. I have found that these children gesture prolifically and in systematic ways, and are impaired in their ability to do arithmetic when they are prohibited from planning motor movements.  Further, by training novice abacus users using iPads, I was able to show that the motor experience of learning the abacus plays a large role in the form of the gestures that users eventually produce.

Please see the "Publications" and "Demos" links to learn more about this work, as well as my other projects on gesture and learning in grade schoolers and adults and on word learning and conceptual development in preschoolers. Much of this work is ongoing, and I am happy to discuss it by email!
