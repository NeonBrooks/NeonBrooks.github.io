Demos


I am a 5th-year Developmental Psychology Ph.D student at the University of Chicago. Through my research, I investigate the ways that we represent complex and abstract information mentally. I am particularly interested in how and when we make use of analog forms of representation like gesture and mental imagery, and how these might work together with symbolic forms of representation like languge.

In my doctoral dissertation, I am working on uncovering the mechanisms by which our hands help us think. I am looking at a particularly useful case study: children in India who solve arithmetic problems using the Mental Abacus technique. These children gesture prolifically, and are impaired in their ability to do arithmetic when they are prohibited from planning motor movements. Because Mental Abacus gestures are not produced with speech or for the benefit of an onlooker, and because the underlying procedures necessary to solve abacus problems are tightly constrained, this case study provides an ideal place to begin exploring the specific roles gesture can play in cognition.

Please see the "Publications" and "Demos" links to learn more about this work, as well as my other projects on gesture and learning in grade schoolers and adults and on word learning and conceptual development in preschoolers.
